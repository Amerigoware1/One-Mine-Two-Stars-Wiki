1. The Principle of Extreme Packing

The core of the system is the Hollow Fiber Membrane. Engineers use bundles of thousands of polymer tubes to maximize the surface-area-to-volume ratio (SA/V).

  • Fiber Scale: Fibers are incredibly fine, often just a few hundred microns in diameter.
  • Packing Density: Commercial modules can achieve packing densities of 45% to 60%, allowing 100 m¬≤ of membrane to fit into a volume the size of a wetsuit‚Äôs dorsal bulge.
  • Material: Highly permeable polymers (like certain silicones or specialized polyimides) allow dissolved oxygen to cross the barrier under a pressure differential.

2. Controlled Flow and Efficiency

The HFM module's structure ensures controlled flow to maximize mass transfer.

Shell-Side Feed (Water Flow): Seawater is pumped into the shell of the dorsal canister, flowing precisely over the exterior of the tightly packed fibers. This high, controlled velocity breaks up the boundary layer‚Äîthe thin layer of low-oxygen water that forms directly on the membrane surface.
Lumen-Side Extraction (Gas Flow): A slight vacuum is maintained inside the hollow center (the lumen) of the fibers. This creates the partial pressure gradient (ΔP) that drives dissolved oxygen to permeate the membrane wall and collect inside the fibers.
The "Wavy" Improvement: While the fibers are fixed, some advanced designs use helical or slightly coiled fibers. This intentional, internal "wave" structure creates beneficial turbulence (Dean vortices), boosting oxygen transfer efficiency.
